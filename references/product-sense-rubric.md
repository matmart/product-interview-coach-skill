# Product Sense Interview Rubric — Detailed Scoring Anchors

## Calibration Note

This rubric is calibrated for **Senior / Staff IC PM** level (Meta E6/E7, Google L6/L7). For Director+ candidates, expect the same structure but with broader strategic framing and ecosystem-level thinking. For mid-career PMs (E5/L5), adjust expectations down — strong segmentation and bottleneck identification are sufficient; ecosystem-level insight is a bonus.

---

## Dimension 1: Structured Thinking

**What it measures**: Does the answer have a clear framework, good transitions, and convergence to a decision?

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| **1** | No visible structure. Jumps between topics. No transitions. Interviewer can't follow the logic. |
| **2** | Attempts structure but loses it mid-answer. Starts organized but drifts into tangents. Transitions are awkward or missing. |
| **3** | Clear framework stated upfront. Follows it mostly, but some sections are uneven (e.g., spends 10 min on motivation, 2 min on solutions). Converges eventually but not cleanly. |
| **4** | Strong framework, well-paced. Each section builds on the previous one. Transitions are explicit ("Now that I've identified the bottleneck, let me generate solutions"). Converges to a clear decision with reasoning. |
| **5** | Framework feels effortless — not mechanical. Candidate navigates between breadth and depth naturally. Returns to their framework when follow-ups pull them off-track. Summaries are crisp. Interviewer always knows where they are. |

**Common failure modes**: Over-expansion without convergence (stays at breadth 3); mechanical framework recitation without genuine thinking (sounds rehearsed); losing the thread when challenged; asking the interviewer for direction too often ("What would you like me to focus on?") instead of driving the conversation.

---

## Dimension 2: User Depth

**What it measures**: Does the candidate demonstrate meaningful segmentation, real empathy, and bottleneck clarity?

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| **1** | No segmentation, or segments by demographics only. No persona. Pain points are generic ("users don't like the experience"). No bottleneck identified. |
| **2** | Attempts segmentation but segments are overlapping or non-mutually-exclusive (same person could fit multiple segments). Persona is a label, not a person. Pain points are listed but not prioritized. Bottleneck is vague. |
| **3** | Meaningful segmentation using a named heuristic. Segments have distinct problems. Persona has some detail but feels like a template. Bottleneck is identified but defense is weak ("it feels like the biggest one"). |
| **4** | Segmentation reveals genuinely different user needs. Persona feels real — named, has a moment of need, has an emotional state. Bottleneck is clearly named and defended with causal reasoning ("if we remove X, Y and Z improve automatically"). |
| **5** | Segmentation is insightful — the interviewer learns something they hadn't considered. Persona is vivid and specific — you can picture this person. Bottleneck identification changes how you think about the problem space. The candidate shows they've thought about this type of user before (or reasons from first principles convincingly). |

**Common failure modes**: Segmenting by demographics (age/gender); all segments having the same pain point; skipping the persona entirely; listing pain points without naming a bottleneck.

---

## Dimension 3: Product Craft

**What it measures**: Is the solution elegant, realistic, ecosystem-aware, and well-articulated?

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| **1** | Solution is vague or unrealistic. No V1 described. No awareness of constraints or tradeoffs. "We should use AI to fix it." |
| **2** | Solution addresses the problem but is generic (any company could propose this). V1 is abstract — no user flow described. Tradeoffs not acknowledged. |
| **3** | Solution is specific to the bottleneck and the company. V1 is described but at feature-list level, not experience level. Some tradeoffs mentioned. Fits the time horizon. |
| **4** | Solution is elegant — it solves the bottleneck in a way that creates additional value. V1 walkthrough feels like a demo ("user opens X, sees Y, taps Z"). Leverages the company's unique assets. What's NOT in V1 is explicitly stated. Tradeoffs are well-reasoned. |
| **5** | Solution makes the interviewer think "I wish we'd built that." V1 is vivid and buildable. Creates a flywheel or network effect. Shows awareness of how the solution interacts with the broader product ecosystem. Tradeoffs include second-order effects. |

**Common failure modes**: Solutions that don't address the stated bottleneck (disconnect); "AI will solve it" without specifics; V1 that's either too vague or too complex for the time horizon; solutions that could work for any company (no right-to-win reasoning).

---

## Dimension 4: Prioritization

**What it measures**: Does the candidate state criteria before choosing, defend "why not" alternatives, and maintain coherence across the segment→problem→solution chain?

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| **1** | No prioritization criteria. Picks segment/solution without explaining why. No "why not" for alternatives. |
| **2** | States a preference but criteria are implicit or post-hoc ("I picked this because it seemed most impactful"). Doesn't address alternatives. |
| **3** | States criteria before choosing. Makes a defensible choice. Mentions alternatives but doesn't deeply defend "why not." Segment→problem→solution chain is coherent but not tight. |
| **4** | Criteria are explicit and reasonable (pain intensity × right to win, or similar). Choice is well-defended. "Why not" is addressed directly: "I didn't pick A because [specific reason]." The chain from segment → bottleneck → solution → metric is airtight. |
| **5** | Prioritization framework is genuinely insightful — reveals how the candidate thinks about tradeoffs. "Why not" defense is strong enough that the interviewer doesn't need to probe. When challenged, the candidate can articulate what would change their mind. The entire answer feels like one coherent argument, not separate sections stitched together. |

**Common failure modes**: Picking a segment "because it feels right" (no criteria); choosing the biggest segment instead of the highest-leverage one; segment-problem-solution disconnect (the solution doesn't address the bottleneck of the chosen segment).

---

## Dimension 5: Creativity

**What it measures**: Are the ideas non-obvious? Does the candidate explore different mechanisms, not just standard playbook thinking?

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| **1** | Solutions are obvious and generic. "Add a search filter." "Send push notifications." No distinctive thinking. |
| **2** | One solution shows some creativity, but the others are standard. Ideas vary by feature, not by mechanism. |
| **3** | Solutions explore different mechanisms (e.g., social proof vs. AI verification vs. financial guarantee). At least one idea is non-obvious. But the creative solution may not be the most feasible. |
| **4** | Multiple solutions are creative AND feasible. The chosen solution has a "that's smart" quality — it's not just different, it's better. The candidate draws on analogies from other products or industries. |
| **5** | The candidate reframes the problem in a way the interviewer hadn't considered. Solutions are inventive but grounded. The V1 has details that show deep product taste — not just "what" but "how" (e.g., the specific UI moment, the copy, the interaction pattern). |

**Common failure modes**: Three variations of the same mechanism; defaulting to "AI will do it" as the creative solution; creativity that's impractical or science-fiction; no creativity at all (pure framework execution).

---

## Dimension 6: Communication

**What it measures**: Is the answer spoken-ready, concise, collaborative, and well-paced?

| Score | Anchor |
|-------|--------|
| **1** | Rambling, disorganized, or silent for long stretches. Interviewer struggles to follow. No summaries or transitions. |
| **2** | Understandable but verbose. Repeats points. Doesn't check in with the interviewer. Sounds like reading an essay, not having a conversation. |
| **3** | Clear and organized. Good transitions between sections. Some filler or repetition but generally concise. Occasionally checks in: "Does this make sense so far?" |
| **4** | Concise and confident. Each sentence adds value. Transitions are natural. Uses strategic pauses and check-ins between sections (waypointing). Summaries are crisp: "So to recap — I'm focusing on Maria, a skeptical buyer whose bottleneck is trust at the decision point." Collaborative tone — responds to interviewer cues. |
| **5** | Sounds like a senior PM thinking live — not rehearsed, not sloppy. Uses precise language. Knows when to go deep and when to stay high-level. Reads the room — adjusts pace and detail based on interviewer engagement. Silence is comfortable, not panicked. |

**Common failure modes**: Over-talking (every section takes 5 minutes); under-talking (rushes through key sections); sounds rehearsed (reciting a framework, not thinking); doesn't respond to interviewer signals (interviewer tries to redirect, candidate ignores it); never checking in with the interviewer between sections.

---

## Scoring Aggregation

| Total Score (sum of 6) | Signal |
|------------------------|--------|
| 25-30 | Strong Hire — exceptional product sense, ready for E6/E7 |
| 20-24 | Hire — solid product sense with minor gaps |
| 15-19 | Mixed — some strengths but significant gaps in 1-2 dimensions |
| 10-14 | Lean No Hire — framework is there but depth or creativity is missing |
| 6-9 | No Hire — fundamental gaps in structured thinking or user empathy |

## Self-Assessment Delta Tracking

After scoring, always compare to the candidate's self-assessment:
- **Over-rater** (>0.5 avg delta above coach score): Candidate thinks answers are stronger than they are. Coach should be more direct in feedback.
- **Under-rater** (>0.5 avg delta below coach score): Candidate undervalues their performance. Coach should reinforce what's working.
- **Accurate** (within 0.5 avg delta): Good calibration. Focus on specific dimension gaps.

Track over time to identify calibration patterns — some candidates over-rate Creativity but under-rate Communication, etc.
